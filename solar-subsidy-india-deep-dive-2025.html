Solar Subsidies in India — Deep Guide (2025)

A practical, sourced guide to PM-Surya-Ghar: process, real delays, cases, and how to protect yourself.

What every homeowner must know

The PM-Surya-Ghar scheme provides central financial assistance for residential rooftop solar, but the subsidy is a post-installation reimbursement that depends on MNRE rules and local DISCOM processes. Delays commonly occur at net-metering, portal processing, or DISCOM approvals — and there are documented cases where approvals or payments were held up for months.

This guide shows exactly how subsidy calculations work, what documents and steps are required, real cases of delays/escalations, and precisely what you should ask installers and DISCOMs to avoid surprises. (Ministry of New and Renewable Energy)

1. The official baseline: what PM-Surya-Ghar / MNRE actually says

Key points from the MNRE guidelines (PM-Surya-Ghar):

  • The scheme is centrally governed by MNRE; DISCOMs implement the process locally through their portals and the national rooftop portal.
  • Subsidy structure is capacity-dependent. For applications on/after specified dates, the guidelines set percentage/amount bands and caps (examples: significant central assistance for up to 2–3 kW with caps beyond 3 kW). The detailed operational guidelines and draft notification are published by MNRE/PM-Surya-Ghar. (View Notification)
What that means for you (plain): MNRE defines who may get money and roughly how much, but the actual cash flow passes through DISCOMs and often requires net-metering, verification, and portal processing before money is released to the consumer’s bank account or the installer (depending on the process).

2. Real numbers & examples (how much you might expect)

MNRE documents and implementation FAQs show subsidy is capped and tiered by system size. Typical structures publicised under PM-Surya-Ghar & related documents include:

  • Substantial central assistance for small residential systems (example: full or large percentage for ≤2 kW; reduced for 2–3 kW; capped beyond 3 kW). Use the MNRE guideline as the legal source for exact percentages/caps for the date of application.
Practical example used by installers: Recent reporting shows central assistance being disbursed in amounts such as ₹30,000–₹78,000 depending on capacity and cut-offs, but market reality (installer prices, local multipliers) means net out-of-pocket varies widely. Some states also add complementary state subsidies. Always check the MNRE guideline for the exact cap that applies to the date of your application. (Mercom India)

3. Step-by-step: How the subsidy process usually flows (and where it breaks)

This is the most important section for homeowners. Follow these steps and keep records at every stage.

Typical pipeline:

  1. Installer & consumer sign an agreement (often using the MNRE model agreement annexure). Keep a copy.
  2. Installation & commissioning (panels, inverter, wiring) — installer must comply with standards and warranties.
  3. Net-meter application submitted (to DISCOM) — this is mandatory for most subsidies. Many DISCOMs have a dedicated net-metering portal or a section on the national rooftop portal. If net-metering application is not filed, subsidies are frequently rejected.
  4. Inspection/verification by DISCOM — physical inspection and technical testing; meter installation (generation meter / net meter).
  5. Subsidy claim submission (installer/consumer via portal) — MNRE/DISCOM cross-verifies and processes the claim.
  6. Subsidy release — usually after verification; timeline can be weeks–months depending on state administrative speed.

Where it breaks (the real delays):

  • Net-metering portal constraints / limits (some portals don’t allow certain capacities or group arrangements yet) — this has blocked approvals.
  • DISCOM processing backlog — there are multiple reports of approvals and subsidy payments taking months. (Telangana Today)
  • Missing or incorrect documents (KYC, ownership proof, sanctioned load mismatch) — these cause rejections or delays. (LinkedIn)

5. State & DISCOM differences

Maharashtra (MSEDCL) — typical issues

MSEDCL requires net-metering application and charges certain application/processing fees in some cases; MERC rules also set normative charges. MERC/MSEDCL documents show processing/inspection fees and prescribed application steps.

Case: Nagpur Metro’s 1.5 MW rooftop project was held up because the DISCOM portal didn’t support updated capacity approvals — MERC issued penalties and ordered MSEDCL to update its portal. This is an example of how portal limitations can block practical approvals. (Times of India)

Delhi (DERC) — group & virtual net metering

DERC issued Group Net Metering & Virtual Net Metering amendments (2024) formalising these mechanisms; Delhi tends to have more advanced policy language but still requires strict adherence to procedural steps. (DERC Guidelines)

Karnataka / Gujarat / Kerala

Gujarat has shown rapid rollout under PM-Surya-Ghar with high uptake. Kerala has examples where the state waived certain fees to accelerate uptake. (KSEB Waiver)

6. Real cases & what they teach us

Case A — Installer reported subsidy delays

What happened: Vendors in multiple states reported that subsidies were pending for months; some consumers only began receiving amounts several months after installation.
Lesson: Expect 1–6 months for subsidy payment. Plan financing accordingly; don’t depend on subsidy as immediate cash.

Case B — Portal limits blocked a big project

What happened: Nagpur Metro’s solar capacity could not be fully approved because MSEDCL portal capped approvals below regulator’s allowed limits. MERC fined MSEDCL.
Lesson: Even large institutional projects can be blocked by portal gaps. For homeowners, this means local procedural readiness matters. Keep a screenshot of your net-meter application as proof.

7. How to ask installers & DISCOMs the right questions

Copy and use these templates.

A. When talking to an installer (before contract)

“Please send me: (1) a written quote with itemised components and labor, (2) the model MNRE contract you will use, and (3) the exact DISCOM portal steps you will complete and whether you will apply for net-metering on my behalf. Also, confirm the timeline you expect from installation to net-meter approval and subsidy claim.”

B. If subsidy is delayed (email to installer + DISCOM)

Subject: Delay in subsidy claim — [Your name] — [Connection/Consumer No]

Hi [Installer name / DISCOM contact],
My rooftop solar system (Ref: [contract no]) was commissioned on [date]. Net-metering application reference number: [ref]. According to MNRE guidelines and the DISCOM portal, subsidy should be processed after verification. Please confirm (a) date subsidy claim was submitted, (b) claim reference number, (c) expected payment date. I would appreciate a written update within 7 working days so I can escalate if needed.
Regards,
[Name, address, contact, consumer number]

If you don’t get a satisfactory reply, escalate to your DISCOM grievance cell and regulator.

8. Escalation paths (who to contact & when)

  1. Installer — first ask for submission proof & claim reference.
  2. DISCOM grievance cell — most DISCOMs have a complaint portal and nodal officer. Use net-metering application ID as evidence.
  3. State regulator (SERC / MERC / DERC) — if DISCOM is non-responsive or delayed beyond regulatory timelines, file a complaint with your state electricity regulator — regulators can impose directions/penalties.
  4. MNRE / Central grievance — for scheme-level issues, MNRE/PM-Surya-Ghar portal listings show contact points.
